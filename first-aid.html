First Aid Guides

Step-by-step instructions for common emergency situations

CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)

Urgent

CPR can help keep oxygenated blood flowing to the brain and other vital organs until emergency medical treatment can restore normal heart rhythm.

Follow These Steps:

  1. Check responsiveness: Tap the person and shout "Are you OK?"
  2. Call emergency services (112): If unresponsive, or ask someone else to call
  3. Position the person: Place them on their back on a firm surface
  4. Begin chest compressions: Place hands on center of chest, push hard and fast (100-120 compressions per minute)
  5. Allow chest to recoil: Let the chest rise completely between compressions
  6. Continue CPR: Until help arrives or person shows signs of life

Note: Hands-only CPR (compression without rescue breaths) is recommended for untrained bystanders.

Choking Relief (Heimlich Maneuver)

Urgent

The Heimlich maneuver can help dislodge an object blocking someone's airway. Use it only when someone is choking and cannot breathe, cough, or speak.

Follow These Steps:

  1. Stand behind the person: Wrap your arms around their waist
  2. Position your hands: Make a fist with one hand, place it thumb-side against middle of person's abdomen, just above the navel
  3. Grasp your fist: With your other hand
  4. Perform quick, upward thrusts: Press hard into the abdomen with quick upward thrusts
  5. Repeat as needed: Continue until the object is expelled or the person becomes unconscious

Note: If the person becomes unconscious, lower them to the ground, call emergency services, and begin CPR.

Stroke Recognition (FAST)

Urgent

A stroke requires immediate medical attention. Use the FAST method to recognize stroke symptoms and act quickly to get help.

FAST Method:

  1. F - Face drooping: Ask the person to smile. Is one side of the face drooping?
  2. A - Arm weakness: Ask the person to raise both arms. Does one arm drift downward?
  3. S - Speech difficulty: Ask the person to repeat a simple phrase. Is their speech slurred or strange?
  4. T - Time to call emergency services: If you observe any of these signs, call emergency services immediately

Note: Other symptoms may include sudden numbness, confusion, trouble seeing, difficulty walking, or severe headache.

Severe Bleeding Control

Important

Severe bleeding can be life-threatening. Acting quickly to control bleeding can save lives until medical help arrives.

Follow These Steps:

  1. Apply pressure: Use a clean cloth or bandage and press firmly on the wound
  2. Maintain pressure: Continue applying steady pressure for at least 15 minutes
  3. Add more bandages: If blood soaks through, add more bandages without removing the original one
  4. Elevate the wound: If possible, raise the injured area above the level of the heart
  5. Apply a tourniquet: For severe limb bleeding that doesn't stop, use a tourniquet if available and you know how to apply it

Note: Always call emergency services for severe bleeding. Do not remove objects embedded in a wound.

Burns Treatment

Important

Proper first aid for burns can reduce pain and severity. The treatment varies based on the type and degree of the burn.

For Minor Burns:

  1. Cool the burn: Hold under cool (not cold) running water for 10-15 minutes
  2. Remove jewelry/tight items: Before swelling occurs
  3. Apply lotion: Use aloe vera or moisturizer
  4. Bandage loosely: Use sterile, non-stick gauze
  5. Take pain reliever: If needed for pain

Note: Seek emergency care for major burns, burns that cover large areas, or burns on the face, hands, feet, genitals, or major joints.

Severe Allergic Reaction (Anaphylaxis)

Urgent

Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially life-threatening allergic reaction that requires immediate emergency treatment.

Follow These Steps:

  1. Call emergency services: Dial 112 immediately
  2. Use epinephrine autoinjector: If available (EpiPen, Auvi-Q, etc.)
  3. Help the person lie down: Raise their legs if possible and loosen tight clothing
  4. Check breathing and pulse: Be prepared to perform CPR if necessary
  5. Additional dose: If symptoms persist after 5-15 minutes and a second autoinjector is available, use it

Note: Common symptoms include hives, swelling, difficulty breathing, rapid heartbeat, and confusion. Don't wait to see if symptoms improve.

Recovery Position

Important

The recovery position helps maintain an open airway for an unconscious person who is breathing normally. It prevents choking and aspiration.

Follow These Steps:

  1. Check responsiveness and breathing: Make sure the person is breathing normally
  2. Position the arm: Place their arm nearest to you at a right angle to their body, elbow bent with palm up
  3. Prepare to roll: Bring their far arm across their chest, and hold the back of their hand against their cheek on your side
  4. Position the leg: Bend their far knee up at a right angle
  5. Roll onto side: Pull on the bent knee to roll them toward you onto their side
  6. Adjust final position: Tilt the head back to ensure the airway remains open

Note: Only put someone in the recovery position if they are breathing normally. If they are not breathing, perform CPR.

Fractures & Sprains

Important

Immediate care for suspected broken bones or sprains can help prevent further injury and reduce pain and swelling.

Follow These Steps:

  1. Stop any activity: Prevent further injury
  2. R - Rest: Avoid putting weight on the injured area
  3. I - Ice: Apply ice wrapped in a thin cloth for 20 minutes several times a day
  4. C - Compression: Use an elastic bandage to reduce swelling
  5. E - Elevation: Raise the injured area above heart level when possible
  6. Immobilize: Use a splint or sling if available

Note: Seek medical attention for significant pain, swelling, deformity, inability to bear weight, or if you hear a snap, grinding, or popping sound.
